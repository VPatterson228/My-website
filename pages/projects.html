Building a New Trail

  • Brush: clear the travel corridor by lopping, sawing or digging out brush that blocks the 2to 4 foot tread width and 8 foot clearing limit of the trail. Dig out organic material too.
  • Excavate: especially on steep hillsides, dig out the flat shelf of the trail into a full bench cutso that the entire width of the trail is on flat terrain. Shape the backslope (uphill side of the trail) into a 45 degree angle and remove any material that might fall onto the trail.
  • Finish: use a shovel or grub hoe to overturn the mineral soil and smooth the tread into a walkable surface, making sure that the trail is slightly outsloped. Pack tread down.

    Building a Turnpike

  • Scrape out mucky area.
  • Dig two parallel trenches and place peeled logs in them, securing them with stakes or rebar.
  • Fill space between the logs with large rocks at the bottom and smaller gravel and mineral soil at the top, forming a crown higher than the logs so water can easily drain off.

    Building a Bridge/Puncheon

    Parts of a Puncheon/Bridge

  • Stringers: long beams that run the length of the bridge and hold up the travel surface.
  • Sills: logs on either end of the bridge, perpendicular to stringers, that stringers rest on.
  • Decking: flat planks on top of the stringers that form the travel surface of the bridge.

    • Types of Bridges

  • Puncheons: boardwalk-like structures, low to the ground, built in boggy areas.
  • Foot logs: single-stringer logs, flattened on top, built on foot-traffic only trails.
  • Multi-stringer bridges: wider bridges built with more than one stringer to accommodate stock or cyclists. Often higher above the water, with hand rails.

    Building Curb Log

  • Excavation: Prepare the area where the curb log will be installed by excavating a trench along the desired edge. The trench should be wide enough to accommodate the curb log and deep enough to provide stability.
  • Foundation: Create a sturdy foundation for the curb log by filling the trench with a layer of compacted gravel or crushed stone. This helps to support the curb log and prevent shifting or settling over time.
  • Installation: Place the curb log into the trench, ensuring that it is level and aligned with the desired edge. Use a level and a mallet to adjust the position of the curb log as needed. If using multiple pieces of material, such as bricks or stones, stack them tightly together to create a cohesive edge.
  • Anchoring: Secure the curb log in place by backfilling the trench with soil or concrete. Compact the backfill material to ensure stability and prevent movement.
